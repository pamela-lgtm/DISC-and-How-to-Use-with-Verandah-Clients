Reading DISC

A shared lens for understanding how each owner thinks, decides, and wants to be treated — so every client feels genuinely met.

The short version

What DISC actually is

DISC is a model for describing behavior and communication style — how a person naturally tends to act, decide, and relate to others. It is rooted in the work of psychologist William Moulton Marston and is used widely in business to help people work together with less friction.

It sorts behavioral tendencies into four styles: D (Dominance), I (Influence), S (Steadiness), and C (Conscientiousness). Everyone is a blend of all four — most people simply lead with one or two.

A few things DISC is not: it is not intelligence, not a measure of how "good" someone is, and not a permanent box. It describes preferences, and people flex depending on the situation. We use it for one reason only — to serve people the way they actually want to be served.

Why it matters here: the same message — a price reduction, a maintenance call, a lease question — lands completely differently depending on who's receiving it. DISC tells us how to frame the message so the owner trusts us and feels at ease.

How it works

Two questions, four styles

The whole model comes down to two questions about how a person operates. First — what's their pace? Fast and outspoken, or measured and reflective. Second — what's their focus? The task and the logic, or the people and the relationship.

Cross those two and you get the four styles. Find where someone sits on the map and you already know a lot about how to approach them.

The four styles up close

What each one values — and how to serve them

D

Dominance

"Just give me the bottom line."

You'll recognize them by

  • Fast decisions, short emails, little small talk
  • Focus on outcomes, control, and ROI
  • Can come across as blunt or impatient

How to serve them

  • Be brief and lead with the result
  • Offer clear options, then let them decide
  • Respect their time — every interaction has a point

Avoid

  • Rambling, over-explaining, or seeming indecisive
I

Influence

"Let's talk it through — I trust you."

You'll recognize them by

  • Friendly, chatty, enthusiastic, optimistic
  • Relationship comes before the fine print
  • May skim details or forget specifics

How to serve them

  • Be warm and personable; let them talk
  • Show genuine enthusiasm for their goals
  • Always confirm key details in writing

Avoid

  • Being cold or all-business; burying them in verbal fine print
S

Steadiness

"Walk me through it — I don't love surprises."

You'll recognize them by

  • Calm, patient, polite, loyal
  • Values consistency and harmony; dislikes change
  • Slow to decide; may not voice concerns

How to serve them

  • Be patient, warm, and sincere
  • Give reassurance and clear, predictable next steps
  • Prepare them gently before any change

Avoid

  • Rushing, pressuring, or springing surprises on them
C

Conscientiousness

"Show me the details and the data."

You'll recognize them by

  • Analytical, precise, careful with facts
  • Asks detailed questions; wants documentation
  • Skeptical of vague or emotional answers

How to serve them

  • Come prepared with specifics, data, and documents
  • Be accurate and exact — say "I'll confirm" over guessing
  • Give them time to review before deciding

Avoid

  • Winging it, being vague, or getting overly gushy
The part everyone asks about

"High C, low S" — reading the blends

Here's the key: a DISC profile scores a person on all four styles at once. So you'll often hear a blend like "high C, low S." That's just describing two of those four scores.

"High" means the trait is strong and shows up often. "Low" means it's not a real driver for them. Reading a blend is just stacking those together.

High = a strong driver

This trait shows up clearly and often. "High C" means precision and accuracy genuinely matter to this person — expect detailed questions.

Low = not a driver

This trait isn't pulling much weight. "Low S" means they're not especially change-averse or slow — they'll move once they're satisfied.

Put those two together and "high C, low S" reads as: a precise, detail-driven owner who wants the facts and the documentation — but who, once satisfied, is comfortable moving and deciding without a lot of hand-holding. Compare that to its near-opposite below.

High C / High S
The careful, cautious owner. Wants every detail and plenty of reassurance and time. Slow to decide, risk-averse, thorough. Give them data, patience, and predictable steps — never a rush. (This is a common, high-touch profile worth recognizing early.)
High C / Low S
The decisive analyst. Just as detail-driven, but quicker to act once the facts check out. Give them the documentation, then let them move. Less hand-holding needed.
High D / High I
The bold connector. Fast, confident, and personable. Wants results and a relationship. Be warm but efficient — energy plus a clear bottom line.
High D / Low I
The all-business driver. Blunt and outcome-focused with little need for warmth or chit-chat. Keep it short, factual, and to the point.
High S / High I
The friendly relationship-builder. Warm, agreeable, and conflict-averse. Lead with connection and reassurance; be gentle delivering anything difficult.

Hold it lightly

A tool, not a label

How we use DISC well

  • It's a starting read, not a verdict. Use it to set your approach, then adjust to the actual person in front of you.
  • People flex. The same owner may be high-D at work and high-S about their property. Watch how they respond and meet them there.
  • Never use it to box someone in. No style is better or worse — each just needs something different from us.
  • Pair it with real listening. DISC sharpens our instincts; it never replaces genuinely paying attention.
